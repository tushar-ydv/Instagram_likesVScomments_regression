Interpretation :

a) Explanation of Endogeneity and its problems:

Endogeneity occurs when an explanatory variable is correlated with the error term in a regression model, which can lead to biased and inconsistent estimates. In the case of our analysis, the two variables at the core of the analysis are Video and FeatureSelf (whether a person features themselves in a post), both of which are likely to be endogenous and here’s why:
1. Simultaneity/Reverse Causality: There could be reverse causality, meaning that the level of engagement (likes or comments) may influence whether a person posts a video or features themselves in a post. For example, a person might decide to post a video or feature themselves because they know that these types of posts receive higher engagement, leading to a reverse relationship between Video and FeatureSelf with engagement. This simultaneity between these variables creates endogeneity, as engagement is affecting the independent variables.
2. Omitted Variable Bias - Confounders: There could be unobserved factors (such as the quality of the content or the time of posting) that influence both the decision to post a video or feature themself, and the engagement metrics (likes and comments). For example, the general appeal of the content (e.g., how interesting or engaging the video is) could be a confounder. If such factors are not included in the regression, then the model is likely to suffer from omitted variable bias, making it difficult to isolate the true effect of videos and feature self posts on engagement.
3. Measurement Error: If there is any imprecision in measuring the Video or FeatureSelf variables (e.g., the categorization of a video post may not be perfectly accurate, or what counts as featuring oneself could be ambiguous), this could cause measurement error, leading to biased and inconsistent estimates.

b) Why the Chosen Instruments Are Good in our model analysis:

The two instruments chosen are Photography and SelfPct (the percentage of the post that features the self in the photo or video). Here’s why they are considered good instruments:
1. Relevance: Both instruments are correlated with the endogenous variables (Video and FeatureSelf).
- Photography is likely to be related to whether a post features a video (as photography and video often go hand-in-hand, especially in content creation).
- SelfPct is related to FeatureSelf, as a higher percentage of the post dedicated to featuring oneself would directly affect whether the post is categorized as featuring the self.
2. Exogeneity:
- Both instruments are assumed to not be directly correlated with the error term of the engagement equation (i.e., not related to unobserved factors influencing engagement). For instance, while Photography may affect whether a post is a video, it is unlikely to directly influence the number of likes or comments, except through its effect on the endogenous variable (Video).
- Similarly, SelfPct is assumed to affect FeatureSelf but not directly affect the engagement metrics unless through the feature self decision.

c) OLS vs. IV Regression Comparison

Let’s compare the results of Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) and Instrumental Variables (IV) regression to highlight how instrumental variables can address certain biases.
OLS or Linear Regression
- Both Video and FeatureSelf show strong, positive relationships with engagement. However, these findings may be biased due to endogeneity.
- The high significance of Video and FeatureSelf suggests they influence engagement substantially, but without addressing endogeneity, their impact may be overstated.
IV Regression results the IV regression, using Photography and SelfPct as instruments for Video and FeatureSelf, provides a different perspective:
1) IV Regression for Likes:
- The impact of Video on likes turns negative (-7.26) compared to the positive result from OLS (2.57), indicating the original OLS estimate was likely biased by endogeneity.
- The influence of FeatureSelf on likes remains large and significant, confirming it drives engagement, though the effect size is somewhat smaller than in the OLS model.
- Followers and LengthChar have similar effects in both models, suggesting they are not endogenously linked to engagement.
2) IV Regression for Comments:
- Video still shows a positive relationship with comments (8.62), though the effect size is smaller than in OLS (11.45), again suggesting that OLS may overstate this effect due to endogeneity.
- The influence of FeatureSelf on comments is smaller than in OLS (5.74 vs 11.90) but remains significant, indicating self-promotion positively impacts engagement, although less so when accounting for endogeneity.

d) In posts where customers complain about companies, how do the factors in the model influence engagement?

In posts where customers complain about companies, several factors influence engagement (likes and comments):
- Followers: Having more followers generally leads to higher engagement, likely because a larger audience sees and interacts with the post.
- LengthChar: Longer posts tend to get more comments, possibly because detailed posts provide more context, encouraging discussion.
- Video: Video posts usually receive more engagement, but this effect may be overestimated in OLS due to endogeneity. With IV regression, videos show a slight positive effect on comments but a negative effect on likes. In complaint contexts, videos may draw more comments but not necessarily as many likes.
- FeatureSelf: Posts featuring the user often get more comments, suggesting that personalizing a complaint post (e.g., showing oneself) makes it feel more relatable and encourages interaction.
- Do customer complaints get more engagement from followers when people post videos, rather than pictures?
- Yes, videos generally receive more engagement compared to pictures, particularly in terms of comments. However, this effect may be overstated in simple OLS analysis due to endogeneity, such as reverse causality or unobserved factors influencing both video use and engagement. After addressing endogeneity through IV regression, videos show a smaller but still positive effect on comments. Interestingly, videos may draw fewer likes compared to pictures, indicating that while videos promote discussion, they may not resonate equally in terms of appreciation (likes).
- Do customers get more engagement when the videos or pictures they post feature themselves (the victim), versus offender (e.g., the airline)?
- Posts that feature the user (the victim) tend to receive more comments, suggesting that personalizing a complaint by showing oneself makes the post more relatable and encourages interaction. This effect highlights the importance of humanizing the complaint to drive engagement. There’s no specific comparison with featuring the offender, but the data suggests that posts with self-featuring content are more effective at generating engagement.

Conclusion:

- Endogeneity: Video and FeatureSelf’s relationship with engagement shows endogeneity, likely due to reverse causality or missing variables.
- IV Instruments: Using Photography and SelfPct as instruments addresses this endogeneity, giving more accurate estimates of how videos and self-featuring impact engagement.
- OLS vs IV: IV regression shows smaller, more reliable effects of Video and FeatureSelf on engagement than OLS initially suggested.
- Customer Complaint Posts: For complaint posts, videos and self-featuring boost engagement, while post length and follower count also significantly increase likes and comments.
